# Cold Calling Across Europe and the US in 2026

## Market-by-Market Connect Rates, and Where You Need a Plan B

Most supply chain software companies we speak to treat outbound as a single workflow. They buy one list, run one script, measure one reply rate, and assume what works in Boston works in Berlin. It doesn't.

Across the last eighteen months we have placed more than 1.1 million cold dials on behalf of logistics, procurement, and supply chain software vendors, in eight markets, in six languages. The connect rates vary by a factor of five.

If you are running one playbook across all of Europe, you are either burning dials in markets where the phone is the wrong channel, or leaving material pipeline on the table in markets where it is still the fastest route to a meeting. This piece walks through what connect rates actually look like in each market, why they differ, and where we recommend clients blend channels rather than dial harder.

---

## What "connect rate" means, and why it is the number to watch

**Connect rate** is the percentage of dials that reach a live human, any human, at the target company. It sits above "conversation rate" (getting the right person on the phone) and well above "meeting booked." It is the first chokepoint in every outbound motion, and it is the metric most sensitive to market, language, and dataset quality.

Industry benchmarks published in the last year by ZoomInfo, Cognism, and Orum put the global B2B connect rate somewhere between three and ten percent, with top performers clearing five. SaaS specifically sits at the low end, with dial-to-meeting conversion under one percent by ZoomInfo's data.

The reality on the ground is that those global averages mask enormous regional spread. Here is what we see.

## UK: the easiest European market to phone into

Fifteen to twenty-two percent connect rates. The UK is the single most responsive European market on the phone, and it is not close. A few reasons:

- A mature direct-dial data layer. Apollo, Cognism, and Lusha all have strong UK coverage, and mobile-to-role mapping is high-quality.
- Cultural willingness to pick up unknown numbers, especially in supply chain, logistics, and freight where operational roles rely on the phone daily.
- English-native targeting removes the language barrier that compresses connect rates in continental Europe.

If you only serve the UK, cold calling is almost certainly your most efficient channel. Most of our UK clients run phone-first with email as a support layer, not the other way around.

## DACH: the highest-leverage European market when you call in German

Eight to thirteen percent connect rates. DACH punches well above its weight on three conditions: the caller speaks native German, the data is clean, and the seniority mapping is right.

German C-level and senior management almost never engage in English on a cold call. They will be polite, they will decline the conversation, and you will burn the dial. In German, the same person will give you three minutes if your opener lands.

This is the single most common failure pattern we see when US software vendors run their own outbound into Europe: they buy a DACH list, dial it in English, get 2 to 3 percent connects, and conclude "cold calling does not work in Germany." It does. Not in English.

## Italy: warmth when you earn it, in Italian

Seven to eleven percent connect rates. Italian buyers are notably more open than their reputation suggests, but only when the call comes in Italian. English-language Italian campaigns we have audited run at half the rate of native-speaker campaigns.

Seniority language matters as much as spoken language. A Direttore Operativo is a genuine decision-maker; a Responsabile Logistica is usually further down. Getting the title layer right before you dial is what turns connects into conversations.

## France: must be in French, full stop

Five to nine percent connect rates. French buyers expect French. If you call a Directeur de la Supply Chain in English, you will get a polite "non merci" or a pass to a junior contact, and you will have burned one of your best-matched leads in the market.

French markets also reward respect for seniority conventions. Directeurs are senior functional heads; Responsables are mid-level; Chefs are line managers. Running a generic Manager-plus filter without this translation produces a list that looks reasonable on paper and underperforms on the phone.

## Spain: local rhythm, local language

Five to eight percent connect rates. Spanish calling works well within a narrower window, typically between 15:30 and 17:30 local time, and almost always in Spanish. Morning calling into Spain is the single most common schedule mistake we see; the hit rate is noticeably lower before lunch.

## Portugal: underserved, rewarding, Portuguese-speaking

Six to ten percent connect rates. A smaller market but one where good native-speaker calling still cuts through, largely because most international SaaS vendors do not staff for it. If Portugal is a real segment in your ICP, the phone is still a viable channel. It will not be forever.

## United States: scale over rate

Five to ten percent connect rates. US connect rates are structurally lower than European benchmarks now, and they are declining year over year. The reason is simple: US carriers and third-party apps (Hiya, RoboKiller, Nomorobo) are aggressively filtering unknown numbers, and every SaaS prospect list is being called by everyone.

Cold calling still works in the US. It works by volume, and it works for teams that invest in local-presence dialling, clean data, and short, specific openers. But "connect rate" is the wrong benchmark to obsess over in the US. Dials-per-meeting-booked is the right one, and that number still holds up.

## Pan-European mixed lists

Eight to fourteen percent connect rates. When a single lead list spans multiple European countries, we route each contact to a native speaker on our team before the dial. The rate above is dial-weighted across those campaigns. The implication is simple: staffing for language is a lever that works even when the list cannot be cleanly segmented by market.

## The Nordics exception: where the phone is the wrong channel

Three to six percent. This is the one market where we actively advise clients not to lead with cold calling. The reasons are structural, not tactical:

**Network-level call filtering.** Telia, which operates across Sweden, Denmark, Finland, and Norway, runs automated fraud and spam filtering at the carrier level. In a single month (January 2022), Telia blocked 4 million calls in Norway, 2.6 million in Sweden, and 2.2 million in Denmark. Sweden also blocks spoofed inbound international calls at the network layer. A meaningful share of your outbound dials never connect, and you have no visibility into which ones.

**Regulatory opt-out registers.** Sweden's NIX register and Denmark's asterisk system in the public directory mean a significant slice of consumer and SOHO numbers cannot be legally called without prior consent. B2B is less restricted, but practical compliance narrows the universe further.

**Cultural skepticism.** Nordic B2B buyers are digitally mature, research-led, and culturally resistant to unsolicited calling. Independent case studies put Nordic cold outreach response rates at roughly a quarter of other European markets.

**Our recommendation.** For Nordic outbound, we run LinkedIn warm outreach and cold email as the primary channels, with phone as a follow-up layer once there is some signal of interest. Community-based GTM (industry events, niche conferences, analyst-led content) produces more pipeline per dollar than a dialler there.

## Why language is the single biggest multiplier

Across every continental European market, in-language calling roughly doubles connect rates versus the same list dialled in English. We see this consistently enough across clients that it is effectively a law: calling a French VP in French, a German Leiter in German, an Italian Direttore in Italian is not a nice-to-have. It is the difference between a campaign that produces pipeline and one that produces invoices.

The operational cost of multilingual outbound is real. You need native-fluent SDRs, not translated scripts. You need multilingual job title scraping, not Anglo-only filters on Apollo. You need market-specific calling windows and local-presence dial numbers. Most agencies that claim "pan-European" coverage do not actually staff for this. It is the thing to audit most carefully when you are picking an outbound partner.

## Where cold calling is not enough: hybrid GTM recommendations

The practical application of all of this, for a CRO or CMO planning outbound across multiple European markets, is that no single channel delivers end-to-end coverage. We recommend clients think in blends:

- **Nordics.** LinkedIn and email primary. Phone as a second-touch channel once a prospect has engaged.
- **Enterprise mobile-only accounts.** Email plus LinkedIn. Mobile spam filtering is a structural headwind; do not fight it.
- **Heavy-compliance verticals (banking, healthcare, public sector).** LinkedIn, events, analyst-led content. Cold calls typically do not clear the gatekeeper.
- **Developer or technical-buyer motions.** Community, product-led signals, and targeted LinkedIn. Cold calling a VP of Engineering gets you nowhere.
- **Everywhere else in Europe.** Phone-first, with email and LinkedIn as reinforcement.

The pattern we see in top-performing programmes: channel mix is chosen per market and per ICP, not applied uniformly across a region.
